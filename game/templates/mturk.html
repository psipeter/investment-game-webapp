Social Behavior in a Web-Based Investment Game

Description (Click to expand)

This study, conducted by researchers at the University of Waterloo (Ontario, Canada) investigates learning and decision-making in an interactive two-player game. As a participant in this study, you will be asked to fill out a short survey, complete a game tutorial, then play several rounds of the ``investment game’’. You may choose to cash-out at any time, receiving bonus payment based on the number of games played.

Study Information (Click to expand)

You are invited to participate in a research study investigating how people learn to play a web-based investment game against various non-human and human opponents. The purpose of the study is to observe the strategies that new and experienced players adopt when playing the game, with the long-term goal of understanding how the brain uses learning to make social decisions. This study is being undertaken as part of Peter Duggins’ PhD research.

What does participation involve?

As a participant in this study, you would be asked to fill out a short survey, watch a tutorial, then play the ``investment game’’ against software opponents. The game involves repeatedly exchanging virtual money with a software opponent, and the goal is to maximizing personal profits. The orientation session requires approximately 20 minutes, and afterwards participants may continue playing games (~60 seconds each) to earn high-paying bonus rewards. Each game move made by the participant will be recorded in the website’s database for research analysis.

What is the Investment Game?

The investment game is a two-player game that involves repeatedly exchanging virtual money. In each round of the game, player A receives $10, then invests some fraction of this capital into player B, keeping the rest. Suppose player A invests $7 and keeps $3. Player B then receives three times the original investment, in this case $21. Finally, player B returns some fraction of this money to player A, say $9. A’s earnings for this round is (10-7+9=12), and B’s earnings is (0+21-9=12). The game repeats for ten rounds, and each player’s final score is their total earnings across all rounds.

In the investment game, greater rewards are earned if both players invest/return generously, but each player will only do so if they trust their opponent to reciprocate in future rounds. For instance, A may always choose to keep the initial capital for a sure “greedy” payoff. However, to obtain the greatest profits, A must risk investing in B. In contrast, B cannot profit if A is greedy, but stands to gain more by being greedy if A does invest heavily. Assessing, predicting, and responding to an opponent’s strategy is thus the central challenge of the game.

Will my identity be known to others?

No identifying information is collected during the course of the study. Participant data is associated with their MTurk ID in a secure database, and only those investigators associated with this study will have access to the records.

Will my information be kept confidential?

The database of participants IDs, survey responses, and game moves will be secured on a password-protected server on the University of Waterloo campus. Research published as a result of data analysis will be submitted to scientific journals, but all user data will remain anonymous. We will keep our study records for a minimum of 7 years, and all records will be destroyed according to University of Waterloo policy.
